Problem & Motivation

NYC subway ridership fell 85% in May 2020. Headlines now point to ridership "bouncing back," but that growth is relative to pandemic lows. Most neighborhoods are still well below where they were in January 2020.

Prior work (Liu 2020, Ziedan 2023, Mahfouz 2024) looks at aggregate city-level trends. None of it connects neighborhood-level ridership recovery to local land use or employment composition.

Research Questions:

  1. How does growth from COVID lows differ from actual return to pre-pandemic baseline?
  2. What neighborhood-level factors predict each type of recovery?
  3. Are bounce-back and true recovery driven by the same predictors, or not?

Acquisition: All data downloaded from public APIs and open data portals. Temporal coverage: Jan 2020 – Dec 2023.

Pipeline: We aggregated hourly station-level ridership to monthly NTA totals by spatially joining 472 stations to NTA polygons. Land-use ratios were computed from PLUTO tax lots. We classified 20 NAICS codes into Remote Work Scores (High/Med/Low) and added demographic variables from the Census ACS.

Final dataset: 133 NTAs × 10 predictors × 2 outcome variables.

Why these methods? We want to know whether neighborhood-level features can predict recovery. Regression gives us interpretable coefficients; ensemble models let us check if the relationship is nonlinear.

Analysis Pipeline:

  • OLS for interpretable coefficients; Ridge & Lasso to handle multicollinearity and feature selection
  • Random Forest & Gradient Boosting to test for nonlinear relationships
  • Validation: VIF screening (max=10.4), 80/20 train-test split, 5-fold CV
  • K-means (K=4, silhouette=0.46) to group NTAs into recovery trajectory types
  • Moran's I to test for spatial autocorrelation in recovery values

Interpretation

Bounce-back (CV R² = 0.54): Education, commercial density, and remote work potential are the top predictors. These areas saw the biggest drops during COVID, so they had the most room to bounce back.

True Recovery (CV R² = 0.18): Our neighborhood features explain very little of the variance. The signal is weak across all model types, suggesting that true recovery depends on factors we cannot measure in public data (e.g., employer RTO policies).

RF Feature Importance:

  • Bounce-back: Education dominates (0.60), followed by income (0.15) and remote work (0.06)
  • True recovery: Importance is spread across race/ethnicity variables (0.17–0.20 each) with no clear dominant feature

Comparison to literature: Forouhar et al. (2025) reported 76% average downtown recovery across 66 North American cities. At the NTA level, we see a similar average but with wide variation (31%–98%), and the features that predict bounce-back do not carry over to true recovery.

Innovations

1

Dual Recovery Metrics: We define and compare two recovery baselines (COVID low vs. pre-COVID) at the NTA level.

2

Remote Work Scoring: We built a weighted scoring system that maps 20 NAICS industry codes to remote work potential, applied at the census block level.

3

Predictive Divergence: We show that bounce-back and true recovery have different predictors and different levels of predictability.

Dual-Map Dashboard: Side-by-side choropleths let users see how the same neighborhood looks different under each recovery definition.

Conclusions & Policy Implications

Key Findings:

  • System-wide ridership sits at 72% of pre-COVID levels; 98% of NTAs are still below their Jan 2020 baseline
  • Bounce-back and true recovery have different predictors and different levels of predictability
  • Neighborhood features explain 54% of bounce-back variance but only 18% of true recovery variance (CV R²)
  • The remaining 28% gap appears to be driven by hybrid/remote work norms that are not captured in public datasets
  • RF and GBM overfit on true recovery (negative CV R²), which rules out the possibility that we are simply undermodeling a complex signal

Policy Implications:

  • Separate the metrics – agencies should track growth from COVID lows and return to baseline independently
  • Plan for uncertainty – true recovery cannot be predicted from neighborhood data alone, so service plans should consider multiple scenarios
  • Watch for equity gaps – essential-worker neighborhoods have steady ridership but low bounce-back ratios, which could lead to underinvestment if agencies prioritize "growth"
  • Revenue models – should account for a persistent ~28% ridership shortfall relative to 2020

Limitations: Analysis covers subway-served NTAs only (133 of 262). Data ends Dec 2023. Employer RTO policies are not measured. Sample size is modest (n=133) for 10 predictors.
